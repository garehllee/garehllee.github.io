SEMESTER 1 @ CMU DESIGN


First-year Fall CMU Design students take two studio courses: Visualizing, a drawing class, and Survey of Design, a projects-based class. This is an archive of my work and process during my First-year Fall.


TIMELINE-

14 Weeks

OVERVIEW

My goal this semester was to develop strong fundamentals and challenge myself in the application of those practices. There were a total of five units across the two studio classes, which ranged from practical/tangible to intellectual/experimental.


1. Devices for Mindful Sensing

[ARTIFACT 1]
Devices for Mindful Sensing

How can Design reconnect people with the intricacies of their surroundings?

We were tasked with designing a “Mindful Sensing Device” meant to re-awaken an assigned sense and enrich users’ understandings of the environments they inhabit. Designs were constrained to be made of string, brown paper, brown tape, wooden dowels, glue, and cardboard.


TIMELINE-

3 Weeks

COURSE-

Survey of Design

ROLE-

Experience/Product Design

Final Deliverable

People typically focus on what is in front of them, especially with the advent of modern technology like phones and computers. The goal of my device is to amplify sound from behind its user and focus attention primarily on hearing.

The spherical dome structure aims to preserve sounds entering from the cone on the back while blocking other noises. The sphere also restricts vision and hand-holds occupy touch to reduce stimuli that can distract from hearing.


Process

I came up with three prototypes for the device. The two non-final designs included a rotating container that would create different noises depending on what went inside and a giant cone that amplified sound on one side of the user’s head.

Ultimately, I opted to go with the dome. After building cardboard prototypes, the container did not produce particularly interesting sounds, and the cone, while effective, did not feel as interesting as the dome. I also felt that I could merge the cone and dome ideas by putting a single large cone on the back of the dome rather than the two originally planned.


In the final sketches, I enlarged the size of the dome and switched to just one cone. As a result of the larger dome, the device no longer fit on the user’s shoulders, so the final build has shoulder pads. Another modification was the addition of a dispersal cone inside of the dome to redisperse the sound captured by the large cone.

From this project, I learned the basics of design process, iteration, and sketching. If I could redo the final design, I would experiment with the shape of the dispersal cone to amplify the sound. I would also improve intuitive usability with the shape language of the shoulder pads and the form itself.


2. Stick/Stone/Leaf

[ARTIFACT 2]
Stick/Stone/Leaf

What can something as simple as a stick, stone, or leaf tell us about its properties, surroundings, and origins?

Using a stick, stone, and leaf picked up without context during a hike, we experimented with several exercises and mediums to capture their essence.


TIMELINE-

4 Weeks

COURSE-

Visualizing

ROLE-

Communication, Processing Information

We used plastic utensils, paper dishes, and acrylic paint to expressively see the stick, touch the stone, hear the leaf, and touch the leaf.


Final Deliverables

For our final compositions, we were asked to use various mediums to explore diagrammatic, representational, expressive, and symbolic representations of our objects. I conducted research into the origins of my objects to inform relational charts.

Stick/stone/leaf was a wild, confusing ride. In one sense, I developed my intuitive and adaptive design instincts. The unit had relatively loose assignments that regularly forced me to use mediums that I was unfamiliar with. Similarly, while we were never taught composition, it became apparent that composition, even during ideation, is an important factor in fully communicating ideas.

I think the most important takeaway from stick/stone/leaf was the development of sketching as a way to think. Just as authors use writing to work through their thoughts, designers draw to explore objects, forms, ideas, and more, developing stories for objects and adjusting ideas to best fit their appropriate use.


3. Butterfly

[ARTIFACT 3]
Butterfly

How can we utilize figure-ground forms to create or convey meaning?

We studied Gestalt Design Principles, specifically figure-ground forms, to create eight 7.25x7.25-inch compositions and a self-directed project based on an assigned butterfly. Explorations included experimentation with positive and negative graphic silhouettes, rotation and form, collage, and color theory.

TIMELINE-

7 Weeks

COURSE-

Survey of Design

ROLE-

Graphic Design

Final Deliverables

Compositions 1 & 2 (positive and negative forms): While other iterations included adjusted forms to increase dynamism, my final forms attempted to be faithful to the original butterfly, especially its curves and roundness. I conveyed movement by adjusting the antenna and abdomen, and rotating the form slightly. I used gouache for the black.

Composition 3 (combine, repeat, and rotate, but do not change size): The goal of this composition is to communicate two butterflies dancing.

Composition 4 (combine, repeat, rotate, and change size): The goal of the composition is to show butterflies on opposite but connected paths, and create a sense of dimensionality.

Compositions 5 & 6 (collage): I began experimenting with colors and textures. On the butterflies, I tried to use color to show light across the composition.

Compositions 7 & 8 (color relationships): I used complementary and light-dark color contrasts.

Final Project (self-directed): I wrote a story about my late grandfather, using butterflies as a symbol. The folded story fits in a sleeve, and unfolds to reveal the next parts.

❮ ❯

Process

Before beginning on forms/counterforms, I did research into my butterfly. I identified it as Catopsilia pomona and took notes on its life cycle, patterns, and behaviors.

Each composition took many iterations. Much of my process involved taking photos of printed butterflies against a background and experimenting with what I liked.

Below is the first draft of the final design.

There were seven drafts of the final butterfly story, each with different colorings and compositions. While the eight butterfly compositions mainly regarded experimentation, the final allowed me to begin infusing meaning into the designs, considering how form could best support the message I wanted to convey. Throughout the project, I learned a ton about form/counterform, composition, color, medium, and technique.

4. Perspective

[ARTIFACT 4]
Perspective

How can we effectively communicate ideas through sketches?

We were taught the fundamentals of one-point and two-point perspective drawing, using cube and cylinder constructions to draw anything and everything from the infamous cubeville to exploding tops to giant hands.

TIMELINE-

6 Weeks

COURSE-

Visualizing

ROLE-

Drawing Fundamentals

Prior to this semester, I had only briefly touched upon perspective and I had not practiced it very much. I also do not have a strong fine-arts background, so drawing has always been challenging. Through this unit, I have become much more confident in my abilities and began to enjoy drawing a lot.

At first, we practiced a lot of cube drawing, then moved onto cylindrical forms. We practiced consistency by using unerasable mediums such as ball point pens, alcohol markers, and fineliners.

5. Stories Imagined

[ARTIFACT 5]
Stories Imagined

How can people of different backgrounds collaborate to create something new?

How can design principles be applied to visual and physical storytelling?

With the Bauhaus Triadic Ballet as inspiration, we co-created a story with Kindergarten students, then put on a play for them, creating sets, costumes, props, and storyboards.

TIMELINE-

4 Weeks

COURSE-

Combined Survey of Design & Visualizing

TEAM-

Amy Choi,
Garrett Lee,
Joy Leung,
Zeqe Golomb

ROLE-

Experience, Performance, Illustration, Visual Identity Design

Final Deliverables

Our final play models consisted of three foreground props, a plane, a bear, a fish with a hospital, a fairy, a cupcake, and some background clouds.

The fish doctor was modeled after a goldfish, and it holds a cupcake as a cure for the upside-down fairy. The fin is worn by the actor on one hand, and the fish itself is worn on the other hand. This way, both the fish and its fin can move.

The bear is built to fit on the actor’s head while the plane goes on the actor’s body. This creates the illusion of the bear inside of the plane and allows for speedy movement.

The fairy can be turned upside-down and fits on the actor’s hand. There is a rainbow sleeve to obscure the actor further.

The props add ambiance to the scene and de-emphasize the actors. By putting something in the foreground, the goal is to create clear visual priority of foreground, middle, and background, which pushes the components of the story into focus over the people controlling the objects.

The performance itself was successful. Overall, the props and set made up a cohesive visual language that was appealing to the kids and properly conveyed the story.

Process

We began with a randomizer game. Each group was assigned a component of a story to create a game for, supposed to randomly select that component for participating kids. For weather, we made a cloud with hanging strips of paper for kids to grab.

After we got our story prompts, we began ideation on characters. Using the framework of a doctor fish, pilot bear, and robot fairy, I iterated on several ideas.

We built some early prototypes. Eventually, we decided that the story had too many components, many of which would take too long to build considering our timeline for the project. This forced us to revamp our entire story and visual identity.

The bear model took a few iterations. We ultimately decided on a trapezoid shape language, which provided thematic contrast with our round fish and square fairy. The viewing hole had to be expanded several times, and the proportions of the head to the body were adjusted to be less intimidating. We chose colors based on their relation to one another as a large color scheme, opting for a darker, more neutral brown for the bear to highlight its purple plane and its contrast with the almost neon-colored fairy.

Storyboards were iterated upon several times as the story changed, especially as shape language was simplified.
