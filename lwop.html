Visualizing Life in Prison Without Parole for Emerging Adults


The visualizer examines extreme Black/White racial disparities in sentencing for Life in Prison Without Parole (LWOP), especially for late adolescents. It aims to begin a conversation about abolishing LWOP with a visually-driven presentation of data surrounding LWOP and race.


TIMELINE-

2 weeks

ROLE-

Researcher & Sole Designer

CONTEXT

Zeroing in on one aspect of an extremely complex issue.

In Fall of 2022, I interned for the Fred T. Korematsu Center for Law and Equality, where I contributed research for an amicus brief recommending the elimination of LWOP for late adolescents (18-21-year-olds) based on racial disparities in sentencing. The brief was submitted to the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court (SJC) in Commonwealth v. Mattis. In January of 2024, the SJC ruled that LWOP for late adolescents is unconstitutional in Massachusetts.

Life in Prison Without Parole (LWOP) is essentially a sentence to death in prison. The practice of LWOP sentencing raises a plethora of moral, sociological, and practical questions about the nature of crime, the purpose of prisons, the treatment of incarcerees, and people’s abilities for reform. In this project, we zero in on statistic-focused data on race and age. Even within the realm of race and age, far more research is needed, especially ethnographic surveys.

In conversations about crime, the emphasis of statistics, particularly false or feeling-based statistics (such as public perception that “crime is on the rise” in 2024, despite crime objectively falling for two consecutive years), highlights our collective tendency to see criminals as subhuman and “other.” At the moment, efforts to humanize people convicted of crimes is rare, but some gems include the Prison Journalism Project and the Marshall Project.


1. Visualizer

VIDEO

Motion graphics and audio to engage viewers and intuitively present data.

Graphics are organized to create a focused narrative. Transitions engage and color and form are used symbolically to represent perceived danger, rigidity, and youth. Graphs and data representations aim to draw on viewers’ intuition to help them understand obscure statistics.

ORGANIZATION

Whiteboard-driven organization. Sticky notes and Illustrator artboards for tight storytelling and engagement.

Using Figma, I color-coded and organized facts and data points. Then, I began to put together a cohesive narrative. Each sticky note was treated as a keyframe for a graphic beat, which I brainstormed on Illustrator then imported into After Effects for animation.
